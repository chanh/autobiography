A Family Memoir in Progress

Thomas & Lily

Interview questions for a memoir of two lives — from Hong Kong to New Jersey

These questions are organized into five levels of depth. Each level covers all the major chapters of their lives — origins, Hong Kong, MIT, meeting each other, career, raising Angela and Jennifer, and legacy — but at increasing levels of detail.

This means a complete and coherent memoir can be written from whatever level they reach. Stop at Level 1 and there is a short but whole story. Reach Level 5 and there is a rich, fully-textured one.

Recording tip: Start each session by asking them to say their full name and today's date. Questions marked Thomas or Lily are best asked separately. Questions marked Both are richest with both present.
Level 1 The Spine — one question per life chapter. Enough for a complete short memoir.
Level 2 The Flesh — key details and context added to every chapter.
Level 3 The Texture — specific memories, named people, turning-point moments.
Level 4 The Grain — sensory details, names and dates, scenes rendered precisely.
Level 5 The Archive — documents, photographs, stories never told, the long tail.
Level 1

The Spine

One or two questions per life chapter. If recording stops here, there is still a complete story — thin but whole, touching every major beat from Hong Kong to today.

Who They Are

  1. Please state your full name, where you were born, and roughly when. Ask each separately. This anchors every recording.
  2. In your own words, tell me the arc of your life — from growing up in Hong Kong to where you are right now — as if you were explaining it to a great-grandchild who knows nothing about you. Don't interrupt. This single answer often becomes the spine of the whole book.

Hong Kong

  1. What was your family like growing up, and what was Hong Kong like as a place?

Coming to America

  1. What brought you to the United States, and why MIT?
  2. What was your experience at MIT like — the challenge, the people, what it gave you?

Meeting Each Other — Both

  1. How did the two of you meet, and how did it become a life together? Ask each separately first — the details often differ memorably.

Building a Life

  1. What did you do after MIT — what was your career, and how did you end up in New Jersey?
  2. Tell me about Angela and Jennifer — who are they, and what do you most want people to know about raising them? Both

Legacy — Both

  1. What do you most want your grandchildren — and the generations after them — to know about who you are and where this family came from?
Level 2

The Flesh

Key facts and context added to every chapter. Stopping here gives a memoir with real substance — each major period of their lives has shape and color.

Origins & Family

  1. Tell me about your parents — who were they, what did they do, and what kind of people were they? Ask each separately.
  2. Did you have brothers and sisters? Tell me briefly about each of them and where they ended up.
  3. Were your parents or grandparents originally from mainland China? If so, what brought them to Hong Kong?

Hong Kong Childhood

  1. What neighborhood did you grow up in, and what was daily life like there?
  2. What was your family's financial situation? Was life comfortable, tight, or somewhere in between?
  3. What language did your family speak at home?
  4. What do you remember most vividly about Hong Kong as a place — the sounds, the smells, the feel of it?

Education & Leaving

  1. What were you like as a student — what subjects drew you, and what did you dream of doing?
  2. Whose idea was it to apply to an American university, and why MIT specifically?
  3. How did your parents react when they found out you were going to America?

MIT

  1. What did you study, and what drew you to that field?
  2. What was the biggest culture shock — at MIT or in America more broadly?
  3. What was the hardest thing about those years?

Meeting & Marrying — Both

  1. Where exactly did you meet, and what were the circumstances?
  2. What was your first impression of the other person? Ask each separately.
  3. Where and when did you get married, and what was the wedding like?

Career & New Jersey

  1. Describe the arc of your career — the fields you worked in, the kind of work you found most meaningful.
  2. Why New Jersey? What made it the place you stayed?
  3. Who were your community in those early years — friends, neighbors, other families from Hong Kong?

Raising Angela & Jennifer — Both

  1. What language did you speak at home when Angela and Jennifer were growing up?
  2. What was the hardest thing about raising children in America while still figuring out the country yourselves?
  3. What are you most proud of in how they turned out?

Identity & Reflection

  1. How do you identify yourself — as Chinese, as Hong Kongese, as American, or some combination?
  2. What do you miss most about Hong Kong?
  3. What is the proudest decision of your life?
Level 3

The Texture

Specific memories, named people, and turning-point moments. This level transforms a factual account into a memoir that feels lived-in.

Origins & Family

  1. Tell me a specific memory of your father — not a general impression, but a particular moment or scene you can still picture. Ask each separately.
  2. Tell me the same about your mother — a specific scene, not a summary.
  3. Do you know the story of how your grandparents came to Hong Kong — what they left behind, and what they were hoping for?

Hong Kong Childhood

  1. What did children do for fun in your neighborhood? What were your favorite things to do?
  2. What was the food like at home? Are there dishes your mother or grandmother made that you still think about?
  3. What was the political atmosphere like when you were growing up — did you have a sense of Hong Kong's relationship to Britain, or to what was happening in mainland China?
  4. What did you imagine your future would look like when you were a teenager?

Education & Leaving

  1. Was there a teacher or mentor who shaped you in a meaningful way — who believed in you or pushed you?
  2. Do you remember the moment you found out you were accepted to MIT? What happened?
  3. What did you pack for a life in a country you had never visited? What did you leave behind?
  4. What were you feeling in the days before you boarded that plane?

MIT

  1. Describe your first day or first week at MIT — the campus, the people, the feeling of arriving.
  2. Who were the friends who mattered most during those years? Are any of them still in your life?
  3. Was there a moment at MIT when you doubted yourself — when you wondered if you could actually get through?
  4. What did MIT teach you beyond the subject matter — about how to think, how to work, how to be?
  5. What is one memory from MIT that you have never forgotten?

Meeting & Marrying — Both

  1. When did you realize it was serious — that this was someone you might spend your life with?
  2. Did you have to work to convince the other person, or was it mutual from the start? Ask each separately.
  3. How did it happen — the proposal?
  4. What did your families think of the match?

Career & New Jersey

  1. What was your first real job? How did you find it, and what was the first day like?
  2. Was there a turning point in your career — a decision, a project, or an opportunity that changed the direction of things?
  3. Did you ever experience discrimination or prejudice in professional life? How did you handle it?
  4. When did you become American citizens, and what did that feel like?

Raising Angela & Jennifer — Both

  1. Tell me your clearest, most specific memory of Angela as a child — a particular moment, not a general impression.
  2. Tell me the same about Jennifer.
  3. How did you transmit Chinese culture to your daughters — through food, through festivals, through discipline, through what you valued?
  4. Were there tensions between what you expected of them culturally and what America expected? How did you navigate that?

Identity & Hong Kong Over Time

  1. How did you feel as the 1997 handover approached? Did it change how you thought about your decision to stay in America?
  2. When you went back to Hong Kong to visit over the years, how did it feel — what had changed, what was the same?
  3. What has surprised you most about how your life turned out — compared to what you imagined when you were young?
Level 4

The Grain

Precise sensory details, specific names and dates, and scenes rendered closely enough that a reader feels present. This level makes the difference between a good memoir and a great one.

Origins & Family

  1. What is the full address — or at least the street or district — of the home you grew up in?
  2. What were your parents' full names, and do you know their birth years?
  3. What did your childhood home look, smell, and sound like? Describe it room by room if you can.
  4. What was a typical weekday morning like when you were ten years old?

Hong Kong

  1. What were the sounds of your neighborhood? What did you hear when you opened a window?
  2. Is there a specific dish — from your mother's cooking, or from a place you went — that still represents Hong Kong to you?
  3. Do you have memories of the 1967 riots, or of the anxiety in Hong Kong during China's Cultural Revolution? How did your family talk about it?
  4. What specific friends did you have as a child? What were their names, what were they like?

Education

  1. What was the name of your secondary school, and what was it known for?
  2. What specific exams or milestones shaped your academic path — and how did you do?
  3. Was there anyone besides you in your family or neighborhood who had gone to study in America? Who were they, and did their experience influence yours?

MIT

  1. What specifically did you study — what was your degree, your thesis or specialization?
  2. What did you do during summers and school holidays when going back to Hong Kong wasn't easy?
  3. Name a professor or advisor who influenced you at MIT. What did they teach you?
  4. What were you eating, where were you living, what did a Saturday look like for you at MIT?

Marriage & Early Life Together — Both

  1. Describe your wedding — who was there, where it was held, what you wore, one moment you remember clearly.
  2. Describe the first home you shared together — the neighborhood, the rooms, what it felt like.
  3. What was a Sunday like for the two of you in your first year of marriage?

Career

  1. What was the name of the first company or institution you worked for? What did the work feel like day-to-day?
  2. What is a professional achievement you are genuinely proud of — something specific, not just a title?
  3. What is the hardest professional challenge you faced, and what got you through it?

Raising Angela & Jennifer — Both

  1. Describe the home Angela and Jennifer grew up in — the neighborhood, the school, the feel of the street.
  2. Tell me a specific funny story about one of the girls — something that still makes you laugh.
  3. Was there a specific moment when you felt the cultural gap between you and your daughters most sharply? What happened?

Hong Kong Connections Over the Years

  1. How did your relationships with your parents change over the decades of living far away — how often did you talk, visit, and eventually say goodbye?
  2. What happened to your siblings — where are they now, and how close are you?
  3. When you visit Hong Kong today, what is gone that you miss? What unexpected thing has survived?

Reflection

  1. If you could sit across from your twenty-year-old self — standing in Hong Kong, about to leave — what would you tell them?
  2. Is there anything you wish you had done differently?
  3. What do you believe about how to live — something you would want to pass down as a kind of family value?
Level 5

The Archive

Documents, photographs, family tree facts, and stories that have never been told. This level ensures nothing is lost — the long tail of a life, gathered before it slips away.

The Family Tree

  1. What are your grandparents' full names — on both sides — and do you know where they were born?
  2. Do you know anything about family from before your grandparents' generation — names, places, stories of any kind?
  3. Are there relatives in Hong Kong, mainland China, or elsewhere in the diaspora that Angela and Jennifer don't know well but should?

Photographs & Documents

  1. Do you have photographs from your childhood in Hong Kong? Can we look at them together and you identify each person?
  2. Do you have photographs from MIT — from your student years?
  3. Do you have your wedding photographs? Are there people in them we should identify and record?
  4. Are there any letters, documents, or objects from Hong Kong that made it to America with you and still exist?
  5. Are there family members in Hong Kong or elsewhere who might hold photographs or documents that we don't have — who should we contact while we still can?

Stories Not Yet Told

  1. Is there a story about your parents or grandparents — something that happened to them — that you have never told your daughters but think they should know?
  2. Is there anything about your own life that you have kept private, that you would now like to have on record?
  3. Is there a person — a friend, a teacher, a colleague, a mentor — who shaped your life profoundly but whose name has never come up in this conversation?
  4. What question do you wish someone had asked you, that this list didn't?
  5. Is there anything you want to add — anything at all — that belongs in the story of your life?
