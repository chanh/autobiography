Chapter I

Origins & Early Life

Our family story begins in two distant corners of Vietnam. Ba — Nguyễn Văn Hiến — was born in 1945, the year of the Rooster, in the village of Tân Long, xã Nhơn An, in Bình Định province. But to understand how the Nguyễn family came to that place, we have to travel much further back — to the ancient imperial capital of Huế.

The family traces its lineage to an ancestor named Nguyễn Phúc Khiêm, a member of the Nguyễn royal house who served as a civil mandarin during the reigns of the emperors Thiệu Trị and Tự Đức in the mid-nineteenth century. He lived in the village of An Cựu, near Huế. At some point — whether by ambition, necessity, or the restless pull of new land — he decided to move south. He made the journey alongside a man called General Trần Đức Hòa, a figure of the same era, and when they reached Bình Định, Nguyễn Phúc Khiêm put down roots there. From him came two sons: the elder, Du, and the younger, Hiệp — who would become Ba's great-grandfather.

It was Nguyễn Hiệp — ông cố — and his wife Trần Thị Lự who established the ancestral home that the family holds to this day. Ông cố was a farmer of means and local standing; his son would later serve as xã trưởng, the village chief. He had befriended a neighbor named Hương Bộ Dư, whose own father had earned a scholarly degree under the French administration and built a fine hardwood house from his fortune. But Hương Bộ Dư had fallen into gambling. As his debts mounted, he began selling what he had. First went the bộ bàn pha — the family's prized tea furniture — and ông cố bought it. Then the losses deepened. Eventually Hương Bộ Dư sold the house itself. Ông cố offered his own thatched home in exchange and took ownership of the old hardwood structure.

With the deal made, the real work began. Ông cố's son — Ba's grandfather, the village chief — used his authority to organize the community: neighbors came together and the old house was carefully dismantled, beam by beam, plank by plank. The pieces were carried to the Nguyễn family's land in Tân Long and reassembled there. That house — acquired through a friend's misfortune and moved by the hands of the community — became the nhà từ đường. In its original form it followed the classic three-room style, ba gian: a central hall flanked by two side rooms, with a separate storage lẫm, a sand courtyard, a side wing, and a well. Generations were born under its roof.

But time and war were not kind to it. Decades passed; bombs fell; fire visited; the wood rotted and the structure buckled. By the time Ba came home from prison in 1981, the house was in serious disrepair. He fixed what he could in those lean years. Then, in 2019, he returned to Bình Định and undertook a second and more thorough restoration. Today the house stands firm again in Thôn Tân Long, xã Nhơn An, quận An Nhơn, Bình Định — restored, and waiting for the grandchildren who may not yet know it is theirs.

Mẹ — Ngô Thị Kim Anh — was born in 1952, in the village of Vạn Khánh, quận Vàng Ninh, in Khánh Hòa province, near the coastal city of Nha Trang. Her childhood was shaped by the sea and by the warmth of ông bà ngoại, in the quiet rhythms of a rural central Vietnamese household — a very different world from the plains of Bình Định where Ba was growing up.

Chapter II

Coming of Age

Ba was born in 1945, but his birth certificate reads 1948. The discrepancy is not an accident. The years of his early childhood — from birth until he was nine — were consumed by the war between France and the Viet Minh, and formal schooling was impossible. It was not until 1954, when Vietnam gained independence, that a child in his situation could finally enter a classroom. By then he was nine years old — too old, by the official reckoning, to begin grade one. So a new birth certificate was drawn up, listing 1948 as his year of birth. Under that name and that date, he started school. He holds two ages: the true one, which made him seventy-eight at the time of this recording, and the official one, three years younger.

Ba completed secondary school during a time when the country had only traded one kind of uncertainty for another. The French were gone but the war between North and South Vietnam was already forming along new lines. For a young man finishing school in those years, there were roughly three paths: continue on to higher study, learn a trade, or join the military. Ba chose the military. More specifically, he chose the Air Force.

The selection process was formidable. Air Force recruitment teams traveled across all four tactical zones of South Vietnam, holding testing sessions in cities and towns along the way. When the team came to Quy Nhơn, Ba registered. More than two hundred young men from the region did the same. From that pool, thirty-five were selected and flown by C-47 transport aircraft to Saigon, where they underwent rigorous medical evaluation at the Air Force's central medical examination center. From those who made it that far, only a handful passed. Ba was one of three men from Bình Định to be accepted. He joined the Air Force in late 1966.

He was assigned to Squadron 62, stationed at the Nha Trang Air Base — the Central Air Force Training Center. Nha Trang was a coastal city, bright and busy, and the base was among the most active in the region. For nearly ten years, through escalating war and shifting fronts, Ba served there. The year 1975 ended that chapter.

Mẹ, meanwhile, had come to Nha Trang to study at the girls' secondary school, boarding at the home of relatives. After finishing, she sat the entrance examination for a teachers' college — a two-year program that would qualify her to teach primary school. She passed, to her own surprise, and enrolled. It was a practical ambition, modest in its scope but steady — and as it would turn out, the teaching credential she earned in those years would sustain her and her family through the hardest decade of her life.

Chapter III

Building a Family

It was 1968 when their paths first crossed. Mẹ was seventeen, enrolled in the teachers' college and boarding at cô Sáu's house in Nha Trang. The household was lively — several young women stayed there: Hường (who would later be known as má Thùy), chị Đắc, and a girl named Xăm who had come from Tú Bông, the same coastal village where Mẹ's own family lived. The house had the energy of shared youth, of young women working toward something and living together in the meantime.

Ba and his fellow airmen from the base would visit. They came around often enough — young men in uniform in a city where the war felt both very close and somehow suspended. Among all the people in that house, it was Mẹ that Ba kept his eyes on. He came back, and came back, paying careful attention to her, the kind of focused noticing that in Vietnamese is simply called tán — to court someone, quietly and steadily, with devotion. Mẹ was not indifferent to it.

In time, Ba made the trip to Tú Bông to meet Mẹ's parents — ông ngoại and bà ngoại. Ông ngoại was immediately impressed. In the countryside in those years, a man who flew military aircraft occupied a particular position in the imagination. The selection standards were so demanding, the numbers accepted so few, that to be an Air Force pilot was considered an achievement of uncommon distinction. Ông ngoại admired this. His approval, offered warmly and without hesitation, was the blessing Ba had come to ask for.

They planned to marry in 1975. The date had been decided, the intention was clear — but the country did not cooperate. In late March, the military situation collapsed with terrifying speed. The Nha Trang Air Base shut down. Ba and Mẹ joined the crowds evacuating by military aircraft, flying south to Saigon in the last days before the end. There they took shelter at the home of cô Chín — Ba's aunt, younger sister of his father's generation, the woman who would become the mother of the cousin the family now knows as chú Triết in Canada.

They waited out the weeks in that house. The harbor filled with ships. On the thirtieth of April, 1975, the last government of South Vietnam surrendered. The ships in the harbor were loaded with people leaving — families, officers, anyone with a way out. Ba could have gone. He had served in the Air Force for nearly ten years; he had reason to fear what came next. But Mẹ would not go. She could not stop thinking about ông ngoại and bà ngoại alone in Tú Bông, and she could not board a ship knowing she might never see them again. Ba looked at the ships and looked at Mẹ. He stayed.

The new government required former officers of the South to report for re-education. They were told the stay would be ten days. Ba reported. He was not released for six years. He was held from 1975 to 1981 — an absence that swallowed the years he had meant to give to a marriage and a family.

Because they had not yet married when Ba was taken, Mẹ's situation carried an accidental fortune — she was technically still a single woman, still a schoolteacher, still employed by the state. The new government's suspicion fell most heavily on the wives of former officers; Mẹ did not formally carry that designation. She kept her teaching position. She was able, when conditions permitted, to make the long journey to the camp where Ba was held — bringing food, supplies, news. These visits, in Vietnamese, are called đi thăm nuôi — going to sustain someone. That is what she did.

Mẹ was not the only one in her family who endured this. Her brother — Cậu Năm — had also served in the South Vietnamese military, as an officer in the finance and payroll branch. He was likewise imprisoned. He was released before Ba, which gave the family at least one set of hands free before the decade was out.

Ba was released in 1981. Ông ngoại did not live to see it — he had died the year before. Ba and Mẹ returned to a small earthen house on what had been ông ngoại's land, in Khánh Hòa. The years that followed were lean. As a former officer of the defeated government, Ba could not obtain meaningful work; the paths that had once been open to him — education, advancement, recognition — were closed. Mẹ's teaching salary was the family's anchor. They lived carefully, in poverty, but they built what they had always meant to build.

There was no wedding. There was no ceremony, no áo dài, no photographs of a couple standing together at the beginning of their life together. That day never came. For people who ask — and the family's children grew up old enough to wonder — this is the answer: the war took it. Not in an abstraction, but specifically: the planning, the date, the preparations, the moment that was meant to happen in 1975 was erased by events too large to argue with. The absence of wedding photographs is not an oversight. It is the precise shape of something that was lost.

One other detail from those years: one of Mẹ's sisters married a man from the North — a soldier named dượng Chính who had come south with the new government. In another family, in another set of circumstances, this might have introduced awkwardness or division. Ba was not bothered by it. Dượng Chính, he noted, had a brother who had served on the South Vietnamese side — which made him a man divided the same way the country itself was divided, and which, in Ba's reckoning, made him trustworthy.

Chapter IV

A New World

The years after Ba's release were quiet and constrained. As a former officer of the old government, Ba was barred from meaningful work. He farmed, found what he could, and waited. Meanwhile, Vietnamese communities already settled in the United States were petitioning the American government on behalf of those left behind — the former soldiers and political prisoners who had endured years of imprisonment. In time, a program was created to answer that call: the Humanitarian Operation, known as HO.

Ba and Mẹ were accepted under HO 26. On June 6, 1994 — twenty-eight years before Ba recorded these words — the family arrived in the United States. The children were small: their daughter was six years old, and little Duyên was just a year and a half. They were sponsored by a generous family and settled in Washington State, drawn there in part because friends — con Ngợi's family — had already made a home in the area. The family's first American roots went down in the city of Tacoma. Three years later, a housing opportunity brought them north to Bellevue, where they have remained.

Ba found work in landscaping — tending lawns and gardens through Washington's seasons. He took pride in it. The work was honest, it connected him to the earth, and in time it gave him enough to give back. He sent money to Vietnam: to help the poor in his home village, to repair roads and bridges, to restore the temple, and above all to rebuild the ancestral home in Tân Long that his great-grandparents had built and that time had worn down.

He also opened the door for others. Cậu Nam Biên came in 2002 and stayed six months; Ba took him across California — north and south — and out to Las Vegas. Dượng Lương came in 2003, and Ba brought him east: to Washington D.C., to the United Nations, to the top of the Empire State Building. Ba sponsored a second visit in 2007, but that stay ended early when Dượng Lương's hand was caught in a lawnmower. American doctors treated him, and he healed, and he returned home to Vietnam — carrying with him a glimpse of the country Ba had worked so hard to make his own.

Chapter V

Legacy & Reflection

On the Fourth of July, 2022, Ba sat before a camera to speak his story. The family had come to California for the holiday — a trip arranged by his son — and there, with the country celebrating around them, Ba began. He said that he wanted his descendants — a hundred years from now, two hundred years — to know where they came from.

He spoke of his full name — Nguyễn Văn Hiến — and of a lineage stretching back to the royal courts of Huế, to an ancestor who traveled south and planted the family name in the soil of Bình Định. He spoke of the ancestral home still standing in Thôn Tân Long, xã Nhơn An — restored and waiting, a place for grandchildren to one day return to and recognize as their own. He said he was the first of the Nguyễn family from Tân Long to live in America; his son, the second generation; and that the generations after that would continue, each one further from the beginning, but connected still.

He had lived his life. He had served his country, survived prison, crossed an ocean, worked the land of a foreign country with his hands, and sent what he earned home. He had kept faith with the people he loved and the place he came from. Everything that needed doing had been done.

Mẹ, too, has her own account to leave for these pages. She was born in 1952, near the sea. She grew up, became a teacher, waited six years for a husband who came home a quieter man, raised her children through years of scarcity, and crossed the world to start again in a place she had never imagined. She has watched her children grow in this country and made it her own. Their story — our story — is written here so that it will not be lost. For the children, and for the children's children, and for whoever comes after. We were here. This is how we lived. This is where we came from.
