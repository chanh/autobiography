Chapter I

Origins & Early Life

Our family story begins in two distant corners of Vietnam. Ba — Nguyễn Văn Hiến — was born in 1945, the year of the Rooster, in the village of Tân Long, xã Nhơn An, in Bình Định province. But to understand how the Nguyễn family came to that place, we have to travel much further back — to the ancient imperial capital of Huế.

The family traces its lineage to an ancestor named Nguyễn Phúc Khiêm, a member of the Nguyễn royal house who served as a civil mandarin during the reigns of the emperors Thiệu Trị and Tự Đức in the mid-nineteenth century. He lived in the village of An Cựu, near Huế. At some point — whether by ambition, necessity, or the restless pull of new land — he decided to move south. He made the journey alongside a man called General Trần Đức Hòa, a figure of the same era, and when they reached Bình Định, Nguyễn Phúc Khiêm put down roots there. From him came two sons: the elder, Du, and the younger, Hiệp — who would become Ba's great-grandfather.

It was Nguyễn Hiệp — ông cố — and his wife Trần Thị Lự who established the ancestral home that the family holds to this day. Ông cố was a farmer of means and local standing; his son would later serve as xã trưởng, the village chief. He had befriended a neighbor named Hương Bộ Dư, whose own father had earned a scholarly degree under the French administration and built a fine hardwood house from his fortune. But Hương Bộ Dư had fallen into gambling. As his debts mounted, he began selling what he had. First went the bộ bàn pha — the family's prized tea furniture — and ông cố bought it. Then the losses deepened. Eventually Hương Bộ Dư sold the house itself. Ông cố offered his own thatched home in exchange and took ownership of the old hardwood structure.

With the deal made, the real work began. Ông cố's son — Ba's grandfather, the village chief — used his authority to organize the community: neighbors came together and the old house was carefully dismantled, beam by beam, plank by plank. The pieces were carried to the Nguyễn family's land in Tân Long and reassembled there. That house — acquired through a friend's misfortune and moved by the hands of the community — became the nhà từ đường. In its original form it followed the classic three-room style, ba gian: a central hall flanked by two side rooms, with a separate storage lẫm, a sand courtyard, a side wing, and a well. Generations were born under its roof.

But time and war were not kind to it. Decades passed; bombs fell; fire visited; the wood rotted and the structure buckled. By the time Ba came home from prison in 1981, the house was in serious disrepair. He fixed what he could in those lean years. Then, in 2019, he returned to Bình Định and undertook a second and more thorough restoration. Today the house stands firm again in Thôn Tân Long, xã Nhơn An, quận An Nhơn, Bình Định — restored, and waiting for the grandchildren who may not yet know it is theirs.

Mẹ — Ngô Thị Kim Anh — was born in 1952, in the village of Vạn Khánh, quận Vàng Ninh, in Khánh Hòa province, near the coastal city of Nha Trang. Her childhood was shaped by the sea and by the warmth of ông bà ngoại, in the quiet rhythms of a rural central Vietnamese household — a very different world from the plains of Bình Định where Ba was growing up.

Chapter II

Coming of Age

Ba grew up during the long conflict between France and the Viet Minh, when schooling was scarce and childhood was shaped by uncertainty. It was not until 1954, after independence, that he first entered a classroom — at the age of nine. He studied hard, completed secondary school, and then faced the choice that defined so many young men of his generation: what path to take in a country that had known only war.

He volunteered for the Air Force. The selection was demanding: from more than two hundred candidates in the Quy Nhơn region, only thirty-five were flown by C-47 transport to Saigon for final screening. Of those, only three from Bình Định were accepted. Ba was one of them. He joined in late 1966, assigned to Squadron 62 at the Nha Trang Air Base — the Central Air Force Training Center — where the planes and the wide sky above the coast became his world.

Mẹ, meanwhile, pursued her own path in Nha Trang. She studied at the city's girls' secondary school, living with her aunt — cô Sáu, the younger sister of Ba's father. After finishing school, she sat the entrance examination for a teachers' college and, to her own surprise, passed. She enrolled in a two-year training program to become a primary school teacher — a calling she would hold onto through the hardest years ahead.

Chapter III

Building a Family

It was 1968 when their paths first crossed. Mẹ was seventeen, living at cô Sáu's house in Nha Trang. Ba, stationed nearby at the air base, had taken to visiting — drawn, like many of his fellow soldiers, by the lively household. But it was Mẹ he kept coming back for. He courted her steadily, as the Vietnamese say, tán — attentive, patient, devoted.

Ba made the journey to Mẹ's home village of Tú Bông to meet ông ngoại and bà ngoại. Ông ngoại was immediately taken with him. In those days, in the countryside, a man who could fly a plane was held in rare esteem — the standards were so rigorous, the training so demanding, that to be accepted as a military pilot was considered something extraordinary. Ông ngoại's warm approval, and Mẹ's own growing affection, sealed what Ba had hoped for.

They planned to marry in 1975. But in the final days of March, as North Vietnamese forces swept south toward Nha Trang, Ba and Mẹ joined the tide of evacuees fleeing by military aircraft to Saigon. They took shelter at the home of cô Chín — Ba's aunt, and the mother of the cousin the family now calls chú Triết in Canada. They waited through April. On the thirtieth of that month, the war ended. South Vietnam was no more.

Ships were waiting in the harbor. Many chose to board them and leave. But Mẹ could not go. She thought of ông bà ngoại, alone in Tú Bông, and her feet would not move toward the water. Ba, who might have boarded a ship and gone to America that very day, chose instead to stay beside her. That choice would cost him six years of his life. As a former officer of the South Vietnamese Air Force, he was taken to a re-education camp. He had been told he would be gone for ten days. It became six years.

Mẹ taught school through those years, living simply, making the long journey to visit Ba whenever she was able. When he was finally released in 1981, ông ngoại had already passed away the year before. They returned to a small earthen house on ông ngoại's land, and the family they had always meant to build — delayed first by war, then by prison — quietly began. Because there was never a ceremony, never a proper day set aside to mark the beginning of their life together, there are no wedding photographs. That is why.

Chapter IV

A New World

The years after Ba's release were quiet and constrained. As a former officer of the old government, Ba was barred from meaningful work. He farmed, found what he could, and waited. Meanwhile, Vietnamese communities already settled in the United States were petitioning the American government on behalf of those left behind — the former soldiers and political prisoners who had endured years of imprisonment. In time, a program was created to answer that call: the Humanitarian Operation, known as HO.

Ba and Mẹ were accepted under HO 26. On June 6, 1994 — twenty-eight years before Ba recorded these words — the family arrived in the United States. The children were small: their daughter was six years old, and little Duyên was just a year and a half. They were sponsored by a generous family and settled in Washington State, drawn there in part because friends — con Ngợi's family — had already made a home in the area. The family's first American roots went down in the city of Tacoma. Three years later, a housing opportunity brought them north to Bellevue, where they have remained.

Ba found work in landscaping — tending lawns and gardens through Washington's seasons. He took pride in it. The work was honest, it connected him to the earth, and in time it gave him enough to give back. He sent money to Vietnam: to help the poor in his home village, to repair roads and bridges, to restore the temple, and above all to rebuild the ancestral home in Tân Long that his great-grandparents had built and that time had worn down.

He also opened the door for others. Cậu Nam Biên came in 2002 and stayed six months; Ba took him across California — north and south — and out to Las Vegas. Dượng Lương came in 2003, and Ba brought him east: to Washington D.C., to the United Nations, to the top of the Empire State Building. Ba sponsored a second visit in 2007, but that stay ended early when Dượng Lương's hand was caught in a lawnmower. American doctors treated him, and he healed, and he returned home to Vietnam — carrying with him a glimpse of the country Ba had worked so hard to make his own.

Chapter V

Legacy & Reflection

On the Fourth of July, 2022, Ba sat before a camera to speak his story. The family had come to California for the holiday — a trip arranged by his son — and there, with the country celebrating around them, Ba began. He said that he wanted his descendants — a hundred years from now, two hundred years — to know where they came from.

He spoke of his full name — Nguyễn Văn Hiến — and of a lineage stretching back to the royal courts of Huế, to an ancestor who traveled south and planted the family name in the soil of Bình Định. He spoke of the ancestral home still standing in Thôn Tân Long, xã Nhơn An — restored and waiting, a place for grandchildren to one day return to and recognize as their own. He said he was the first of the Nguyễn family from Tân Long to live in America; his son, the second generation; and that the generations after that would continue, each one further from the beginning, but connected still.

He had lived his life. He had served his country, survived prison, crossed an ocean, worked the land of a foreign country with his hands, and sent what he earned home. He had kept faith with the people he loved and the place he came from. Everything that needed doing had been done.

Mẹ, too, has her own account to leave for these pages. She was born in 1952, near the sea. She grew up, became a teacher, waited six years for a husband who came home a quieter man, raised her children through years of scarcity, and crossed the world to start again in a place she had never imagined. She has watched her children grow in this country and made it her own. Their story — our story — is written here so that it will not be lost. For the children, and for the children's children, and for whoever comes after. We were here. This is how we lived. This is where we came from.
