Chapter I

Roots

Our family story begins in two distant corners of Vietnam. Ba — Nguyễn Văn Hiến — was born in 1945, the year of the Rooster, in the village of Tân Long, xã Nhơn An, in Bình Định province. But to understand how the Nguyễn family came to that place, we have to travel much further back — to the ancient imperial capital of Huế.

The family traces its lineage to an ancestor named Nguyễn Phúc Khiêm, a member of the Nguyễn royal house who served as a civil mandarin during the reigns of the emperors Thiệu Trị and Tự Đức in the mid-nineteenth century. He lived in the village of An Cựu, near Huế. At some point — whether by ambition, necessity, or the restless pull of new land — he decided to move south. He made the journey alongside a man called General Trần Đức Hòa, a figure of the same era, and when they reached Bình Định, Nguyễn Phúc Khiêm put down roots there. From him came two sons: the elder, Du, and the younger, Hiệp — who would become Ba's great-grandfather.

It was Nguyễn Hiệp — ông cố — and his wife Trần Thị Lự who established the ancestral home that the family holds to this day. Ông cố was a farmer of means and local standing; his son would later serve as xã trưởng, the village chief. He had befriended a neighbor named Hương Bộ Dư, whose own father had earned a scholarly degree under the French administration and built a fine hardwood house from his fortune. But Hương Bộ Dư had fallen into gambling. As his debts mounted, he began selling what he had. First went the bộ bàn pha — the family's prized tea furniture — and ông cố bought it. Then the losses deepened. Eventually Hương Bộ Dư sold the house itself. Ông cố offered his own thatched home in exchange and took ownership of the old hardwood structure.

With the deal made, the real work began. Ông cố's son — Ba's grandfather, the village chief — used his authority to organize the community: neighbors came together and the old house was carefully dismantled, beam by beam, plank by plank. The pieces were carried to the Nguyễn family's land in Tân Long and reassembled there. That house — acquired through a friend's misfortune and moved by the hands of the community — became the nhà từ đường. In its original form it followed the classic three-room style, ba gian: a central hall flanked by two side rooms, with a separate storage lẫm, a sand courtyard, a side wing, and a well. Generations were born under its roof. From this household came Ba's father, Nguyễn Hội — and from Nguyễn Hội, Nguyễn Văn Hiến.

But time and war were not kind to it. Decades passed; bombs fell; fire visited; the wood rotted and the structure buckled. By the time Ba came home from prison in 1981, the house was in serious disrepair. He fixed what he could in those lean years. Then, in 2019, he returned to Bình Định and undertook a second and more thorough restoration. Today the house stands firm again in Thôn Tân Long, xã Nhơn An, quận An Nhơn, Bình Định — restored, and waiting for the grandchildren who may not yet know it is theirs.

Chapter II

Two Childhoods

Ba was born in 1945, but his birth certificate reads 1948. The discrepancy is not an accident. The years of his early childhood — from birth until he was nine — were consumed by the war between France and the Viet Minh, and formal schooling was impossible. It was not until 1954, when Vietnam gained independence, that a child in his situation could finally enter a classroom. By then he was nine years old — too old, by the official reckoning, to begin grade one. So a new birth certificate was drawn up, listing 1948 as his year of birth. Under that name and that date, he started school. He holds two ages: the true one, which made him seventy-eight at the time of this recording, and the official one, three years younger.

Ba completed secondary school during a time when the country had only traded one kind of uncertainty for another. The French were gone but the war between North and South Vietnam was already forming along new lines. For a young man finishing school in those years, there were roughly three paths: continue on to higher study, learn a trade, or join the military. Ba chose the military. More specifically, he chose the Air Force.

Mẹ — Ngô Thị Kim Anh — was born in 1952, in the village of Vạn Khánh, quận Vàng Ninh, in Khánh Hòa province, near the coastal city of Nha Trang. Her childhood was shaped by the sea and by the warmth of ông bà ngoại, in the quiet rhythms of a rural central Vietnamese household — a very different world from the plains of Bình Định where Ba was growing up.

Chapter III

The Air Force

The selection process was formidable. Air Force recruitment teams traveled across all four tactical zones of South Vietnam, holding testing sessions in cities and towns along the way. When the team came to Quy Nhơn, Ba registered. More than two hundred young men from the region did the same. From that pool, thirty-five were selected and flown by C-47 transport aircraft to Saigon, where they underwent rigorous medical evaluation at the Air Force's central medical examination center. From those who made it that far, only a handful passed. Ba was one of three men from Bình Định to be accepted. He joined the Air Force in late 1966.

He was assigned to Squadron 62, stationed at the Nha Trang Air Base — the Central Air Force Training Center. Nha Trang was a coastal city, bright and busy, and the base was among the most active in the region. For nearly ten years, through escalating war and shifting fronts, Ba served there. The year 1975 ended that chapter.

Mẹ, meanwhile, had come to Nha Trang to study at the girls' secondary school, boarding at the home of relatives. After finishing, she sat the entrance examination for a teachers' college — a two-year program that would qualify her to teach primary school. She passed, to her own surprise, and enrolled. It was a practical ambition, modest in its scope but steady — and as it would turn out, the teaching credential she earned in those years would sustain her and her family through the hardest decade of her life.

Chapter IV

How They Met

It was 1968 when their paths first crossed. Mẹ was seventeen, enrolled in the teachers' college and boarding at the nhà đèn — cô Sáu's house in Nha Trang. The household was lively — several young women stayed there: Hường (who would later be known as má Thùy), chị Đắc, and a girl named Xăm who had come from Tú Bông, the same coastal village where Mẹ's own family lived. The house had the energy of shared youth, of young women working toward something and living together in the meantime.

Ba and his fellow airmen from the base would visit. They came around often enough — young men in uniform in a city where the war felt both very close and somehow suspended. Among all the people in that house, it was Mẹ that Ba kept his eyes on. He came back, and came back, paying careful attention to her, the kind of focused noticing that in Vietnamese is simply called tán — to court someone, quietly and steadily, with devotion. Mẹ was not indifferent to it.

In time, Ba made the trip to Tú Bông to meet Mẹ's parents — ông ngoại and bà ngoại. Ông ngoại was immediately impressed. In the countryside in those years, a man who flew military aircraft occupied a particular position in the imagination. The selection standards were so demanding, the numbers accepted so few, that to be an Air Force pilot was considered an achievement of uncommon distinction. Ông ngoại admired this. His approval, offered warmly and without hesitation, was the blessing Ba had come to ask for.

Chapter V

April 30, 1975

They planned to marry in 1975. The date had been decided, the intention was clear — but the country did not cooperate. In late March, the military situation collapsed with terrifying speed. The Nha Trang Air Base shut down. Ba and Mẹ joined the crowds evacuating by military aircraft, flying south to Saigon in the last days before the end. There they took shelter at the home of cô Chín — Ba's aunt, younger sister of his father's generation, the woman who would become the mother of the cousin the family now knows as chú Triết in Canada.

They waited out the weeks in that house. The harbor filled with ships. On the thirtieth of April, 1975, the last government of South Vietnam surrendered. The ships in the harbor were loaded with people leaving — families, officers, anyone with a way out. Ba could have gone. He had served in the Air Force for nearly ten years; he had reason to fear what came next. But Mẹ would not go. She could not stop thinking about ông ngoại and bà ngoại alone in Tú Bông, and she could not board a ship knowing she might never see them again. Ba looked at the ships and looked at Mẹ. He stayed.

The new government required former officers of the South to report for re-education. They were told the stay would be ten days. Ba reported. He was not released for six years. He was held from 1975 to 1981 — an absence that swallowed the years he had meant to give to a marriage and a family.

Chapter VI

The Lost Years

Because they had not yet married when Ba was taken, Mẹ's situation carried an accidental fortune — she was technically still a single woman, still a schoolteacher, still employed by the state. The new government's suspicion fell most heavily on the wives of former officers; Mẹ did not formally carry that designation. She kept her teaching position. She was able, when conditions permitted, to make the long journey to the camp where Ba was held — bringing food, supplies, news. These visits, in Vietnamese, are called đi thăm nuôi — going to sustain someone. That is what she did.

Mẹ was not the only one in her family who endured this. Her brother — Cậu Năm — had also served in the South Vietnamese military, as an officer in the finance and payroll branch. He was likewise imprisoned. He was released before Ba, which gave the family at least one set of hands free before the decade was out. Cậu Năm's wife — Mợ Năm — had followed a path remarkably parallel to Mẹ's: she too had attended teachers' college and qualified to teach Grade 1. She had been teaching in Biên Hòa, but when Cậu Năm came home she applied to transfer, following her husband back to Tú Bông to teach near family. By the time Ba was released in 1981, Cậu Năm and Mợ Năm already had four children: chị Thư, anh Dũng, anh Thụ, and the youngest, Thái — born just the year after ông ngoại died.

Ba was released in 1981. Ông ngoại did not live to see it — he had died the year before. Before his death, ông ngoại had made provisions for his children. He divided his garden land: the front portion, where the family home stood, he left to Cậu Năm's family. At the back of the garden he built a small house for Ba and Mẹ — a nhà đất, an earthen house, humble in its materials but solid in its intention. That is where Ba returned after six years away. The years that followed were lean. As a former officer of the defeated government, Ba could not obtain meaningful work; the paths that had once been open to him — education, advancement, recognition — were closed. Mẹ's teaching salary was the family's anchor. They lived carefully, in poverty but not in despair, and together they built what they had always meant to build.

There was no wedding. There was no ceremony, no áo dài, no photographs of a couple standing together at the beginning of their life together. That day never came. For people who ask — and the family's children grew up old enough to wonder — this is the answer: the war took it. Not in an abstraction, but specifically: the planning, the date, the preparations, the moment that was meant to happen in 1975 was erased by events too large to argue with. The absence of wedding photographs is not an oversight. It is the precise shape of something that was lost.

One other detail from those years: one of Mẹ's sisters married a man from the North — a soldier named dượng Chính who had come south with the new government. In another family, in another set of circumstances, this might have introduced awkwardness or division. Ba was not bothered by it. Dượng Chính, he noted, had a brother who had served on the South Vietnamese side — which made him a man divided the same way the country itself was divided, and which, in Ba's reckoning, made him trustworthy.

Chapter VII

A New World

The years after Ba's release were quiet and constrained. The new government did not trust former officers of the old one, and the distrust was expressed in very practical ways. Ba could not find meaningful work. The positions that drew on his skills — his years of training, his service record, his discipline — were closed to him. He farmed. He found odd jobs. He waited. The family lived on Mẹ's teacher's salary, which was small. These were years of compression, of holding still while the country rebuilt itself around them in a shape that left little room for people like Ba.

The way out came from across the ocean. Vietnamese who had already settled in the United States — veterans' communities, diaspora organizations, people who had escaped in 1975 and established themselves in American cities — began to advocate for those left behind. They petitioned the American government. Specifically, they argued for the former political prisoners: the men who had reported for re-education and spent years in camps. The American government eventually responded, and a program was created to allow these men and their immediate families to emigrate. It was called the Humanitarian Operation, abbreviated HO.

Ba and Mẹ were accepted under the twenty-sixth cohort of that program — HO26. On June 6, 1994, their family arrived in the United States. The children were small: their eldest, born in 1988, was six years old. Their younger daughter, Duyên, was a year and a half. They carried almost nothing. They were met and sponsored by a generous family — the Ev Châu family — and they came to Washington State in part because a trusted family they knew, con Ngợi's family, had already settled there. Their first American address was in Tacoma.

Three years after arriving in Tacoma, a housing assistance opportunity became available further north, in Bellevue. The family moved. Bellevue, a prosperous suburb east of Seattle, became their permanent home — the city where the children grew up, where Ba found his footing, where the family's American life took its settled shape.

Ba found work in landscaping. He tended lawns and gardens — a man who had once maintained aircraft now keeping grass trimmed and flowerbeds neat through Washington's long gray winters and brief green summers. He kept at it for twenty-five, twenty-six years before retiring. He was not embarrassed by the work. He was proud of it. It was honest labor, it was skilled labor in its own way, and over time it gave him something that the constrained years in Vietnam never could: enough.

With enough, he gave back. He sent money to Vietnam regularly — to help the poorest families in Tân Long, to repair village roads and bridges, to restore the communal temple. And, most importantly, to rebuild the ancestral home. The nhà từ đường had been standing since his great-grandparents' generation. Time and neglect had worn it down. Ba had it repaired once in the years after his imprisonment. Then, in 2019, he undertook a full restoration — the most significant work done to the house in generations. The house that exists today in Thôn Tân Long, xã Nhơn An, quận An Nhơn, Bình Định is the house his great-grandfather received in that old gambling debt, carried beam by beam to its current location by the village chief and his neighbors, and now preserved by a man who had traveled to the other side of the world and still sent his earnings home.

He also used what he had built in America to bring his family over, one by one. Bác Tám came first — that sponsorship was completed successfully before Ba turned his attention to the next. Cậu Nam Biên arrived in 2002 and stayed six months. Ba drove him across California — north to San Francisco, south to San Diego, and out to Las Vegas in the desert — showing him a country of enormous distances and stranger landscapes than either of them had grown up imagining. Dượng Lương came in 2003 and also stayed six months. Ba took him east: to Washington D.C. to see the monuments, to the United Nations building in New York City, up to the observation deck of the Empire State Building. For a man who had grown up in Khánh Hòa and spent much of his adult life in constrained circumstances in postwar Vietnam, these were sights that must have seemed improbable.

Dượng Lương came again in 2007. He worked for a few months. Then there was an accident with a lawnmower — the same kind of machine Ba had spent years operating — and the blades caught his hand, cutting through his fingertips nearly to the base of the nails. He was taken to an American hospital. The treatment was thorough; American medicine handled the injury with resources and care that would not have been available to him in Vietnam. He recovered, and he returned home to Khánh Hòa, carrying a scar and, presumably, a story.

After Dượng Lương's second visit in 2007, both Bác Tám and Cậu Năm expressed interest in coming as well. But Ba weighed the circumstances and decided against it. Bác Tám had already made one trip; a second long visit at his age no longer seemed wise, and there was a deeper concern: the ancestral home in Tân Long. With everyone abroad, the nhà từ đường would be left unguarded — and Ba worried about what might happen to the property in the family's absence. Cậu Năm's health had also deteriorated; he was not strong enough for the journey. The sponsorships stopped there. Ba had brought over who he could.

Chapter VIII

Legacy & Reflection

On the Fourth of July, 2022, Ba sat before a camera to speak his story. His son had bought plane tickets and brought the family to California for the holiday — a vacation, a gathering, and, as it turned out, an occasion for something more lasting. Ba chose that day to record himself. He addressed his descendants directly — not the children in the room with him, but the ones he would never know: the great-grandchildren, the ones a hundred years from now, two hundred years. He wanted them to know who he was and where they came from.

He began with his name. His full name is Nguyễn Văn Hiến. He was born in 1945 — truly 1945, though the official record says 1948. He has lived through the end of the Franco-Vietnamese War, the entirety of the American-Vietnamese War, the fall of the South, six years of imprisonment, the slow constrained years that followed, and then, at forty-nine years old, the crossing to America. He is, by his own account, the first of the Nguyễn family from Tân Long to live in the United States — the ông tổ, the founding patriarch, of the family's American branch. His son is the second generation. The generations that follow will be the third, the fourth, each one further from Bình Định, but descended from the same line.

He spoke of the ancestral home as an anchor for all of this. It stands at Thôn Tân Long, xã Nhơn An, quận An Nhơn, tỉnh Bình Định. It is a nhà từ đường — a clan hall, a house of the ancestors, a place that belongs to the family collectively and across time. Ba had it restored so that it would be there when his descendants came looking. He wanted future generations, no matter how far they had traveled or how many years had passed, to be able to go to that address, walk through that gate, and know: this is where we started. This is the place.

He also described the lineage itself, so that the record would be complete. The Nguyễn family traces its origin to Nguyễn Phúc Khiêm, a civil mandarin who served under the emperors Thiệu Trị and Tự Đức in Huế, living in the village of An Cựu. In that period, a general named Trần Đức Hòa was sent south to govern Bình Định. Nguyễn Phúc Khiêm accompanied him. In Bình Định, he had two sons: the elder, Du, and the younger, Hiệp — who would become Ba's great-grandfather. It is from Hiệp that the family in Tân Long descends.

Mẹ, too, has her account to add. Her full name is Ngô Thị Kim Anh. She was born in 1952 in Vạn Khánh, Khánh Hòa, near the sea. She grew up, studied, became a teacher — and then history arrived, and she waited six years for a man who came home quieter than he had left. She raised her children through years when there was very little. She crossed the Pacific at the age of forty-two and has now lived in America for more than twenty-eight years. She has made it her own: the grocery stores, the seasons, the grandchildren's school events. This country, in the way that only practical daily life can accomplish, has become hers.

Ba said that everything that needed doing has been done. He has served, suffered, worked, built, given back, and recorded. Now there is time to rest, to travel where he likes, to follow the season. He said this without regret and without complaint. He had done his part. The story continues with the people who come after — with the son who bought the plane tickets, with the daughter who was a year and a half old when she arrived in Tacoma, with the grandchildren who have never known a Vietnam at war and have never had to choose between the ships in the harbor and the parents in the village. This is the life those choices made possible. This is what was built.
